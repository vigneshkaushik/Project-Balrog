## Anchor constraint

Sprinkler pendant positions are governed by fire code — maximum head spacing, maximum distance from walls, maximum distance from obstructions. Interior ceiling module must accommodate these requirements. The fire code governs; the interior design adapts.

## Recommended resolutions (ranked)

### 1. Align sprinkler positions to ceiling module within code tolerance

**Effort:** Low.

Sprinkler pendant positions have a tolerance band of typically ±150–200mm from the hydraulically ideal position, within which they can be adjusted to align with the ceiling tile/panel module or intersection. Fire protection engineer must confirm adjusted positions are code-compliant for the specific hazard classification. Confirm pendant drop arm length suits the ceiling board thickness at each position.

**Checks:**

- [ ] Fire protection engineer confirmation that all adjusted positions are within code coverage requirements
- [ ] Pendant drop arm length confirmed at each position (varies with ceiling thickness)
- [ ] Ceiling tile/panel has a cut-out or pre-drilled hole for pendant — confirm tolerance

### 2. Specify concealed or recessed sprinkler heads

**Effort:** Low.

In premium areas, upgrade to concealed sprinkler heads (flush-mounted with flat chrome or painted escutcheon plate). Concealed heads sit flush with the ceiling surface and are invisible when installed. Requires confirming that the specific head type is approved by the fire authority for the occupancy and hazard classification of the space. Specify escutcheon finish to match ceiling colour or material.

**Checks:**

- [ ] Fire authority approval for concealed head type in this occupancy classification
- [ ] Concealed heads provide adequate coverage for the room geometry (some have reduced coverage areas)
- [ ] Escutcheon finish spec (colour match, chrome, custom paint) included in MEP specification

### 3. Fire engineering solution for complex ceiling geometry

**Effort:** High. **VO risk:** Yes (formal variation / redesign likely).

For geometries (coffered, vaulted, stepped, with deep beams) where standard sprinkler rules produce unacceptable visual results or position constraints, commission a performance-based fire engineering solution. Options include: sidewall heads at wall level, extended coverage heads, or alternative suppression (only with AHJ agreement). Fire engineer prepares a performance-based fire design report for authority submission.

**Checks:**

- [ ] Fire engineer engagement confirmed and fee agreed
- [ ] Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) pre-consultation completed on the alternative approach
- [ ] Additional cost and programme time for performance-based design acceptance estimated and approved

## Validate before acting

- [ ] Obtain preliminary sprinkler layout drawing (hydraulic design) before interior ceiling design is finalised
- [ ] Confirm occupancy/hazard classification of each space — this drives head type, spacing, and discharge density
- [ ] Confirm ceiling type in each area — accessible tiles vs. fixed plasterboard affects head selection significantly

## Escalate immediately if

- Feature ceiling obstructs sprinkler pattern coverage and cannot be modified — fire engineering alternative is mandatory before interior design can proceed
- Interior design requests sprinkler-free zones — requires AHJ pre-approval and alternative suppression or fire engineering justification

## Downstream risk if unresolved

Sprinkler and interior ceiling coordination is consistently left too late. Any change to sprinkler head positions after hydraulic calculations are finalised requires recalculation and potentially additional heads. Coordinate before hydraulic design is issued for approval — not after.
