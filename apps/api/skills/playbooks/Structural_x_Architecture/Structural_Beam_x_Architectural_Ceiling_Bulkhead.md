## Anchor constraint

Structural beam is immovable in elevation. Architectural ceiling is the flexible element — ceiling level, bulkhead depth, or reflected ceiling plan (RCP) can be adjusted. The resolution must be agreed between structural, architectural, and MEP teams together, not unilaterally.

## Recommended resolutions (ranked)

### 1. Lower ceiling locally to accommodate beam soffit

**Effort:** Low.

Adjust the architectural ceiling level at this location to sit below the beam soffit + minimum clearance (typically 50mm for movement + ceiling board thickness + finish). In BOH areas, a local step in the ceiling is standard. For FOH areas, coordinate with the interior designer to incorporate the step as a designed feature — a bulkhead reveal, cove light, or shadow gap can transform a forced step into an architectural feature.

**Checks:**

- [ ] Headroom below revised ceiling meets code minimum (typically 2400mm habitable, 2100mm corridors)
- [ ] Revised ceiling line coordinated with lighting, diffusers, and sprinkler positions
- [ ] If FOH: architect/ID sign-off on design treatment for the ceiling step

### 2. Expose beam as architectural feature

**Effort:** Low.

In exposed or semi-exposed areas, remove the ceiling at the beam location and design the beam as a feature — exposed concrete or painted/cased steel within the ceiling composition. Eliminates the clash by removing the ceiling at the beam. Coordinate paint spec, fireproofing visibility (intumescent paint can be left exposed if spec allows), and MEP services concealment strategy.

**Checks:**

- [ ] Architect approval for exposed beam treatment
- [ ] Fire protection method is acceptable for exposed condition
- [ ] All MEP in zone either concealed or also treated as an exposed installation

### 3. Revise beam profile to tapered or castellated section

**Effort:** High. **VO risk:** Yes (formal variation / redesign likely).

In cases where beam soffit elevation is critical to multiple spaces, propose a castellated (perforated web) or tapered beam profile that reduces beam depth at mid-span. This is a structural change requiring SE redesign. MEP services can also route through castellated beam web openings (designed penetrations), resolving both beam-ceiling and beam-MEP clashes simultaneously. Only viable early in design.

**Checks:**

- [ ] SE to redesign beam profile as castellated or tapered
- [ ] Check if castellated web openings align with MEP service routes
- [ ] Confirm revised beam depth clears full MEP services stack below

## Validate before acting

- [ ] Confirm beam soffit elevation from structural drawings — allow ±10mm RC construction tolerance
- [ ] Identify if area is FOH or BOH — determines aesthetically acceptable resolution approach
- [ ] Identify all services to be concealed above ceiling — beam acts as a zone divider affecting service layout on both sides

## Escalate immediately if

- Beam soffit drops headroom below minimum code requirement — architectural design must change at design level
- Clash affects a series of beams across a floor plate — indicates fundamental misalignment between structural grid and ceiling design intent that cannot be resolved clash by clash

## Downstream risk if unresolved

Beam-ceiling clashes resolved by locally lowering the ceiling in multiple locations create irregular ceiling planes that cannot be coordinated with lighting and diffuser layouts post-facto. Issue a revised RCP after every ceiling-level change and re-coordinate all ceiling-mounted MEP elements in the affected zone.
